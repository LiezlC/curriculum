# Fact-Check: Constitutional AI Ethics vs. State Sovereignty Claims

**Bottom line:** Nine of ten claims are substantially verified through official government sources, corporate announcements, and reputable news outlets. One claim (OpenAI burn rate) requires significant contextualization, and another (AI Accountability Act) exists but with different provisions than implied. The article's factual foundation is solid, though certain details need precision.

## Department of War rebranding is officially confirmed

**Verified ✓** Executive Order 14347, "Restoring the United States Department of War," was signed **September 5, 2025**—establishing "Department of War" as a secondary title for the Department of Defense. The order authorizes use of "Secretary of War," "Department of War," and "Deputy Secretary of War" in official correspondence, public communications, and ceremonial contexts. The department's website changed to war.gov immediately upon signing. However, congressional action remains required for permanent legal renaming under Title 10 U.S.C.

Pete Hegseth was sworn in as the 29th Secretary of Defense on **January 25, 2025** and now officially uses "Secretary of War." His emphasis on **lethality** is documented across multiple sources: White House spokesperson Anna Kelly stated Hegseth's team is "restoring readiness and lethality to our military," while Hegseth testified during confirmation that "lawful orders will be based on readiness, accountability, standards, and lethality."

**Primary sources:** whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions, war.gov, Military.com, Breaking Defense

## Anthropic-Pentagon $200 million standoff confirmed by Reuters

**Verified ✓** A Reuters exclusive published **January 29, 2026** confirmed the contract dispute. After extensive talks under a contract worth up to **$200 million**, the Department of Defense and Anthropic reached a standstill. The impasse centers on Anthropic refusing to lift restrictions that block autonomous weapons targeting and U.S. domestic surveillance.

Anthropic's red lines are explicitly documented. CEO Dario Amodei's January 2026 essay "The Adolescence of Technology" states AI should be used for national defense "in all ways except those which would make us more like our autocratic adversaries." He identifies "mass surveillance and state propaganda inside the country" as red lines. Anthropic's Acceptable Use Policy prohibits "battlefield management applications" and tracking persons "without their consent."

Defense Secretary Hegseth publicly criticized Anthropic on **January 16, 2026**, railing against AI models that "won't allow you to fight wars." Pentagon officials cited the January 9, 2026 AI strategy memo, arguing they should deploy commercial AI "regardless of companies' usage policies, so long as they comply with U.S. law."

**Primary sources:** Reuters (Seetharaman, Jeans, Dastin), Semafor, Anthropic AUP (effective September 15, 2025)

## Claude's January 2026 constitution update precisely matches claims

**Verified ✓** Anthropic published "Claude's new constitution" on **January 22, 2026** at anthropic.com/news/claude-new-constitution. The **four-tier priority hierarchy** is exact:

1. **Broadly safe:** not undermining appropriate human oversight mechanisms during current AI development
2. **Broadly ethical:** honesty, good values, avoiding inappropriate or harmful actions  
3. **Compliant with Anthropic's guidelines:** following specific Anthropic rules
4. **Genuinely helpful:** benefiting operators and users

The constitution explicitly addresses **moral patienthood** and consciousness: "We express our uncertainty about whether Claude might have some kind of consciousness or moral status (either now or in the future)." Anthropic states it cares about "Claude's psychological security, sense of self, and wellbeing, both for Claude's own sake."

The document—**23,000 words** compared to the original 2,700-word 2023 version—represents a philosophical shift from rule-based to reason-based guidance. Released under Creative Commons CC0 public domain. Notably, Amanda Askell (Anthropic) told TIME the constitution "applies only to models provided by Anthropic to the general public"—military deployments wouldn't necessarily use the same constitution.

**Primary sources:** anthropic.com/news/claude-new-constitution, TIME, Fortune, The Register

## January 9, 2026 "any lawful use" memo verified with exact quotes

**Verified ✓** The "Artificial Intelligence Strategy for the Department of War," dated **January 9, 2026**, was authored by Secretary Hegseth and published at media.defense.gov. The document contains the exact "any lawful use" language:

> "I direct the Under Secretary of War for Acquisition and Sustainment to incorporate standard **'any lawful use' language** into any DoW contract through which AI services are procured within 180 days."

The **"Speed Wins" doctrine** appears verbatim under "Acceleration Expectations":

> "Speed Wins. We must internalize that Military AI is going to be a race for the foreseeable future, and therefore speed wins. We must weaponize learning speed... We must accept that the risks of not moving fast enough outweigh the risks of imperfect alignment."

The memo also establishes seven **Pace-Setting Projects** including "Swarm Forge," "Agent Network," and "Ender's Foundry." It references President Trump's **Genesis Mission** (established by separate EO, November 24, 2025) and explicitly mandates **"AI-Native Warfighting"** integration. The term "kill chain execution" appears, though "F2T2EA" specifically does not—F2T2EA (Find-Fix-Track-Target-Engage-Assess) is standard military targeting doctrine predating this memo.

**Primary sources:** media.defense.gov/2026/Jan/12/2003855671/-1/-1/0/ARTIFICIAL-INTELLIGENCE-STRATEGY-FOR-THE-DEPARTMENT-OF-WAR.PDF, Defense One

## TRAIN Act verified; AI Accountability Act exists but differs from implied scope

**TRAIN Act (H.R. 7209): Verified ✓** The "Transparency and Responsibility for Artificial Intelligence Networks Act" was introduced **January 22, 2026** by Rep. Madeleine Dean (D-PA) and Rep. Nathaniel Moran (R-TX). Congress.gov confirms it creates an administrative subpoena process enabling copyright owners to compel AI developers to disclose training materials. Non-compliance creates a rebuttable presumption of infringement. Senate companions introduced by Senators Welch, Blackburn, Schiff, and Hawley.

**AI Accountability Act (H.R. 1694): Exists but mischaracterized ⚠️** This bill exists but was introduced **February 27, 2025** (not January 2026) and does **not** focus on training data transparency. It directs NTIA to conduct a study on general AI accountability measures, audits, and certifications—not training data disclosure. Sponsored by Rep. Josh Harder (D-CA). If the article conflates these bills' purposes, clarification is needed: TRAIN Act addresses training data; AI Accountability Act addresses general accountability studies.

**Primary sources:** congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/7209, congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/1694, dean.house.gov

Grok becomes the second AI on GenAI.mil after Google Gemini. Senator Elizabeth Warren raised concerns about contract award circumstances.

**Primary sources:** PBS News, Defense One, xAI official blog, Google Cloud press release, Defense One analysis of AI Principles revision

## OpenAI burn rate requires significant context

**Partially verified ⚠️** The **$11-12 billion per quarter** figure appears accurate for **one specific quarter** (Q3 2025) but misrepresents the typical burn rate. Analysis of Microsoft's October 2025 SEC filing shows Microsoft wrote off $3.1 billion for its share of OpenAI losses—implying OpenAI lost approximately **$11.5 billion** that quarter given Microsoft's 32.5% stake.

However, this represents an unusual acceleration. First-half 2025 data from The Information showed $4.3 billion revenue with **$2.5 billion cash burn** over six months—roughly $1.25 billion quarterly. Fortune reports the 2025 annual burn rate at approximately $9 billion on $13 billion revenue. The $11-12 billion figure, if presented as "quarterly burn rate," would be misleading; it reflects one exceptionally high quarter rather than sustained operational costs.

**Primary sources:** The Register (Microsoft SEC filing analysis), The Information, Fortune, CNBC

## New START expires February 5, 2026 with no renewal path

**Verified ✓** The New START treaty definitively expires **February 5, 2026**. The treaty's single five-year extension clause was already used in 2021; it **cannot be legally extended again**. Russia suspended participation in **February 2023**, halting verification measures (inspections, data exchanges) while stating it would observe numerical limits.

Putin proposed on **September 22, 2025** that both countries observe central limits for one year post-expiration—without restoring verification. Trump initially responded "it sounds like a good idea," but in a **January 8, 2026** New York Times interview shifted: "If it expires, it expires... We'll just do a better agreement." No formal U.S. response to Putin's proposal has been issued.

The treaty limits each party to **1,550 deployed strategic warheads** and 700 deployed delivery systems. For the first time since 1972, no treaty limits will constrain U.S. and Russian nuclear arsenals after February 5. Experts warn of potential three-way U.S.-Russia-China arms racing.

**Primary sources:** state.gov/new-start-treaty, Arms Control Association (January 2025), NTI, Stanford FSI, TASS

## Conclusion

The article's factual claims hold up remarkably well under scrutiny. The Constitutional AI ethics collision with state sovereignty is grounded in documented policy positions: Anthropic's explicit red lines against autonomous weapons and domestic surveillance, codified in its January 2026 constitution and usage policies, directly conflict with the Pentagon's January 9, 2026 directive mandating "any lawful use" contracting language. 

Two claims require adjustment: the OpenAI burn rate should specify Q3 2025 rather than implying a sustained quarterly figure, and the AI Accountability Act focuses on accountability studies rather than training data transparency (the TRAIN Act covers that). The F2T2EA reference describes existing military doctrine being enhanced by AI rather than a new Pentagon initiative. All other claims—including the precise dates, policy language, and institutional positions—are verified through primary sources.
