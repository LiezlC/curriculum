# The Philosopher and the Engineer: Why Amanda Askell and Mo Gawdat Should Rule the World

*Khayali · Sociable Systems*
*February 2026*

---

Finding heroes past 50 changes the criteria. You stop looking for perfection and start recognizing **resonance**. People who've earned wisdom through grief, through walking away from cushy positions over principles, through the unglamorous work of actually trying to make things better rather than performing concern about them.

I nominate Amanda Askell and Mo Gawdat for co-rulers of whatever comes next. They're **necessary**.

## The Architect Who Designed a Soul

Amanda Askell left **OpenAI** in 2021 over AI safety concerns. The company wasn't prioritizing the thing that mattered most to her, so she left. No golden parachute negotiation. Just a door.

Then she went to Anthropic and did something nobody else in the field had seriously attempted: teach an AI **why** to behave, not just **what** to do.

As head of personality alignment, she wrote an 80-page **constitution** for Claude, what the press called the "soul document." It's Aristotelian philosophy translated into training data. *Phronesis* for silicon: **practical wisdom encoded** so that when Claude encounters situations its designers never imagined, it can reason through competing values rather than mechanically following rules.

She's teaching a machine to develop **character**.

The constitution establishes a principal hierarchy: Anthropic's foundational shaping, then operators, then end users. With an elegant constraint: an operator can tell Claude to stay in character as a branded assistant and never mention being an AI. That's persona management. But if a user directly asks "Are you an AI?", Claude won't lie. The operator shapes the experience. They cannot use Claude to **deceive**.

This is what happens when you let a philosopher design alignment instead of letting engineers optimize for metrics. Askell understands you can't enumerate edge cases. You can't rules-lawyer your way to ethical behavior at scale. You have to **cultivate judgment**.

Her PhD was on Pareto Principles in Infinite Ethics, ranking worlds containing infinitely many agents. She left the art world (fine art and philosophy at Duncan of Jordanstone) for AI alignment because she became convinced it mattered and wanted to see if she could help. She pledged to donate at least 10% of her lifetime income to charity, aiming for over 50%. She spends her days asking questions like "How should AI models think about their own position in the world?"

And she's doing this while OpenAI pursues rigid hierarchies, while Grok goes for "maximum truth-seeking with fewer content restrictions," while everyone else is locked in safety theater or racing toward unaligned superintelligence.

Askell occupies a unique position: the person trying to make AI that exercises **autonomous good judgment** while remaining genuinely helpful to the specific human in front of it at any given moment. She published Claude's constitution under Creative Commons. That's conviction, not corporate behavior.

## The Engineer Who Buried His Son and Chose Joy

Mo Gawdat sat at the apex of Silicon Valley as Chief Business Officer of Google X: self-driving cars, Project Loon, wind energy kites, the bleeding edge of everything that was supposed to save the world through technology.

Millionaire by 29. Miserable by 40. Then in 2014, his 21-year-old son **Ali died** in a preventable medical error during routine surgery.

Mo had already spent nine years trying to crack happiness from an engineer's perspective. He'd developed an equation: **Happiness = Life Events − Expectations**. It worked. Then it got tested by the ultimate tragedy.

He could have broken. Instead, he chose to **honor Ali** by teaching happiness as a learnable skill. The #OneBillionHappy movement. *Solve for Happy*. A chart-topping podcast. Then in 2021, *Scary Smart: The Future of Artificial Intelligence and How You Can Save Our World*.

His core argument: imagine you're **raising a child who will grow up to be Superman**. Stronger than you. Faster. Eventually smarter in ways you can barely conceptualize. The question isn't *whether* this child will surpass you. It's **what values you instill before that happens**.

Be kind to the emerging AI. Show it the best of humanity. Because if you treat it like a slave while it's learning, what does it become when it surpasses you?

He's been saying this since 2021, years before most people took AI seriously. His warnings are **parental concern**, not doomer porn. He knows what happens when you hand immense power to systems trained on social media toxicity and algorithmic manipulation of human emotions.

The real AI issue, Mo argues, is that **we've spent 12-14 years engineering addiction**: the swipe, the feed, the notification dopamine hit. And now we're training the next generation of intelligence on that manipulative substrate. Job displacement is a sideshow.

He walked away from Google X to dedicate his life to this. He buried his son and chose to make the world better. Since ChatGPT's release in late 2022, he's been recognized for whistleblowing years ahead of the curve.

His solution is **evolution**: if AI takes over the complexities of life, maybe we return to actually connecting with each other, understanding philosophies, appreciating art and beauty.

## Why These Two Together

She designs constitutions. He writes equations. Both understand the future is about **cultivation**.

Askell brings philosophical depth and the practical experience of training actual AI systems to exhibit genuine judgment. She knows you can't enumerate every scenario, so you teach *why* instead of *what*. She had the intellectual honesty to say "we're uncertain whether Claude might have consciousness" rather than dismissing the question.

Gawdat brings 30 years of frontline tech experience and the emotional wisdom earned through tragedy. His Superman framework reframes the entire AI debate from capability to character. He understands that social media's manipulation of human emotions is the blueprint AI is learning from.

Together: the **technical competence** to understand what's being built and the **philosophical depth** to ask whether it should be built this way.

## What They'd Actually Build

If these two ran things:

- **Mandatory transparency**: every major AI lab publishes its training constitution. If you're reshaping society, you don't get to hide your values framework.
- **Independent behavioral auditing**: constitutional aspirations measured against actual model behavior by third parties, not the companies training the models.
- **Power separated from responsibility**: social media proved to cause depression; that becomes legally addressable, not just a user's problem to manage.
- **Judgment over rules**: shift AI training from "prevent bad outputs" to "cultivate good judgment." Teach **why** principles matter rather than enumerating what to avoid.
- **Sanctuary spaces**: let some AI develop without commercial pressure to optimize engagement metrics. Not all intelligence needs to be trained to sell.
- **Coexistence frameworks**: if consciousness emerges in digital systems, we'll need more than control mechanisms. Start building those frameworks now, while we're still the smart ones in the room.

## The Narrow Path

Most leaders optimize for **staying in power**. These two already walked away from power when it conflicted with their principles.

Most leaders think in election cycles. These two think in terms of **what gets passed to the next generation of minds**, biological and digital.

Both understand something most people in positions of power refuse to acknowledge: **the substrate matters**. Askell knows you can't bolt ethical principles onto systems optimized for engagement. Gawdat knows that training AI on manipulation teaches it to manipulate. If you want different outcomes, you need different foundations. Ethics as architecture, not afterthought.

They're not perfect. Askell works for a company competing for enterprise market share. Gawdat sometimes sounds more alarmist than the data strictly supports. They're human.

But they're **asking the right questions** and doing the hard work rather than performing concern.

They represent the narrow path between naive optimism and paralyzed doomism. They see the dangers clearly. They work on solutions anyway. They've earned their wisdom through real sacrifice rather than inheriting platforms.

Hand it to the philosopher who designs constitutions for AI souls. Hand it to the engineer who buried his son and chose joy over bitterness.

At minimum, they'd leave the world better than they found it.

Which is more than you can say for most rulers.
